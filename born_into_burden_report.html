Born Into Burden

How unequal access to handwashing shapes life expectancy and demographic pressure. Across the matched 2022 sample, countries with the weakest access also tend to carry the heaviest burden — and live the shortest lives.

UNICEF Data Analysis | 2024 | 103 Countries in the matched 2022 sample

This report’s central finding is simple: low handwashing access does not sit alone. It clusters with higher dependency ratios and shorter life expectancy. The pattern is visible on the map, confirmed in the scatterplot, and reinforced by the gap between low-access and high-access country groups.

The Problem We Refuse to See

Access to soap and water is often treated as a minor household convenience. The data suggest the opposite. In the matched 2022 sample, countries with the weakest handwashing access also tend to have the highest dependency burdens and the shortest lives.

The matched data make that inequality concrete with one stark comparison: Liberia records only 3.4% handwashing access and life expectancy of about 61.9 years, while Qatar reaches 100% access and life expectancy of about 81.9 years. Same planet, radically different starting line.

What this report tests: whether that headline inequality holds across the wider dataset. The answer is yes. Figure 1 shows where low access is concentrated, Figure 2 quantifies the life expectancy penalty, Figure 3 shows how dependency burden moves with hygiene access, and Figure 4 shows why progress remains too slow in the most affected region.

The Data

The analysis combines three UNICEF and World Bank files linked through ISO alpha-3 country codes. The objective is to create one comparable 2022 cross-section for the map, bar chart, and scatterplot, and a longer time series for regional trend analysis.

  • Handwashing access (unicef_indicator_1): share of the population with a handwashing facility (soap and water) available at home, 2000–2024, 120 countries.
  • Total dependency ratio (unicef_indicator_2): dependents (children under 15 and adults 65+) per 100 working-age adults, 1950–2023, 228 countries.
  • Country metadata (unicef_metadata): life expectancy at birth, GDP per capita, population, and other contextual variables, 1960–2024.

After filtering to the most recent year with broad overlap and removing countries with missing life expectancy, the working cross-sectional dataset covers 103 countries. That overlap is large enough to support strong comparisons while keeping the joins transparent and reproducible.

import polars as pl # Load all three datasets ind1_raw = pl.read_csv("unicef_indicator_1.csv") # handwashing ind2_raw = pl.read_csv("unicef_indicator_2.csv") # dependency ratio meta_raw = pl.read_csv("unicef_metadata.csv") # metadata # Handwashing: keep "Total" sex, rename columns hw_all = ( ind1_raw .filter(pl.col("sex") == "Total") .select(["country","alpha_3_code","time_period","obs_value"]) .rename({"obs_value":"handwashing_pct","time_period":"year"}) ) # Dependency ratio: Total only, 2022 dep_2022 = ( ind2_raw .filter((pl.col("time_period")==2022) & (pl.col("sex")=="Total")) .select(["alpha_3_code","obs_value"]) .rename({"obs_value":"dep_ratio"}) ) # Life expectancy: 2022, drop nulls le_2022 = ( meta_raw .filter(pl.col("year") == 2022) .select(["alpha_3_code","Life expectancy at birth, total (years)"]) .rename({"Life expectancy at birth, total (years)":"life_exp"}) .drop_nulls("life_exp") ) # Inner join all three — 103 countries with complete data merged_2022 = ( hw_all.filter(pl.col("year")==2022) .join(dep_2022, on="alpha_3_code", how="inner") .join(le_2022, on="alpha_3_code", how="inner") .with_columns( pl.col("handwashing_pct") .cut(breaks=[15.0,40.0,65.0,85.0], labels=["Q1 (0-15%)","Q2 (15-40%)","Q3 (40-65%)", "Q4 (65-85%)","Q5 (85-100%)"]) .alias("hw_band") ) ) print(f"Final dataset: {merged_2022.shape[0]} countries")

A World Divided: Geographic Distribution of Handwashing Access

Figure 1 makes the geography of inequality visible. The lowest handwashing access is concentrated across Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia, while high-access countries dominate Europe, the Gulf states, and much of Latin America. This is not a random pattern; it follows deeper differences in infrastructure, income, and public-health capacity.

import geopandas as gpd from plotnine import * # Load world shapefile try: world_gdf = gpd.read_file(gpd.datasets.get_path("naturalearth_lowres")) except AttributeError: world_gdf = gpd.read_file( "https://naciscdn.org/naturalearth/110m/cultural/ne_110m_admin_0_countries.zip" ) # Merge handwashing data into GeoDataFrame world_map = world_gdf.merge( merged_2022.select(["alpha_3_code","handwashing_pct","hw_band"]).to_pandas(), left_on="iso_a3", right_on="alpha_3_code", how="left" ) # Assign access-band labels and plot MAP_FILL = { "Q1 (0-15%)":"#d73027","Q2 (15-40%)":"#fc8d59", "Q3 (40-65%)":"#fee090","Q4 (65-85%)":"#91cf60", "Q5 (85-100%)":"#1a9850","No data":"#cccccc", } p1 = ( ggplot(world_map) + geom_map(aes(fill="hw_band"), color="white", size=0.12) + scale_fill_manual(values=MAP_FILL, name="Access Level") + theme_void() + labs(title="Global Handwashing Access, 2022", caption="Source: UNICEF/WHO JMP") ) p1.save("viz1_world_map.png", dpi=150)

The geography is stark. Sub-Saharan Africa is almost entirely in the bottom two access bands — red and orange across a continent of over a billion people. South Asia occupies the lower-middle band. Meanwhile, North Africa, the Middle East, and most of Europe and Central Asia sit comfortably in the top band.

In Liberia — the worst-performing country in this dataset — only 3.4% of the population has a handwashing facility at home. Bahrain and Qatar sit at 100%. These two countries share the same planet, the same pathogens, and roughly the same year — but almost nothing else about the public health infrastructure available to their citizens.

The 10-Year Life Sentence

Figure 2 turns that spatial divide into a measurable life expectancy penalty. Average life expectancy rises steadily as handwashing access improves, moving from the low 60s in the weakest-access group to the mid-70s in the strongest-access group. The gradient is too consistent to dismiss as coincidence.

from plotnine import * import pandas as pd q_order = ["Q1 (0-15%)","Q2 (15-40%)","Q3 (40-65%)","Q4 (65-85%)","Q5 (85-100%)"] # Compute average life expectancy per access band qs = ( merged_2022 .group_by("hw_band") .agg(pl.col("life_exp").mean().alias("avg_life_exp")) .to_pandas() ) qs["hw_band"] = qs["hw_band"].astype(str) qs = qs.set_index("hw_band").reindex(q_order).reset_index() qs["bar_label"] = qs["avg_life_exp"].round(1).astype(str) + " yrs" qs["hw_band"] = pd.Categorical(qs["hw_band"], categories=q_order, ordered=False) global_avg = float(merged_2022["life_exp"].mean()) QUINTILE_COLOURS = { "Q1 (0-15%)":"#d73027","Q2 (15-40%)":"#fc8d59", "Q3 (40-65%)":"#fee090","Q4 (65-85%)":"#91cf60","Q5 (85-100%)":"#1a9850", } p2 = ( ggplot(qs, aes(x="hw_band", y="avg_life_exp", fill="hw_band")) + geom_col(width=0.65, show_legend=False) + geom_hline(yintercept=global_avg, linetype="dashed", color="#444", size=0.75) + geom_text(aes(label="bar_label"), va="bottom", nudge_y=0.5, size=10, fontweight="bold", color="#1D3557") + scale_fill_manual(values=QUINTILE_COLOURS) + scale_x_discrete(limits=q_order) + scale_y_continuous(breaks=[50,55,60,65,70,75,80]) + coord_cartesian(ylim=(50, 80)) + theme_minimal() + labs(title="The 10-Year Life Sentence", x="Handwashing Access Quintile", y="Average Life Expectancy (years)") ) p2.save("viz2_bar_chart.png", dpi=150)

Grouped into five handwashing-access bands, the 103 countries show a clear upward gradient: average life expectancy rises from about 63.8 years in the lowest-access group to about 74.0 years in the highest-access group. In other words, the countries at the top of the hygiene distribution outlive those at the bottom by roughly a decade.

Read together with Figure 1, the message is sharper: the life expectancy penalty is not abstract. It is built into the same countries where basic household protection is weakest. For a public-facing story, this is the report’s strongest evidence that handwashing access functions as a proxy for wider development conditions.

The Dependency–Hygiene Nexus

Figure 3 adds a third layer to the story. Countries with high dependency ratios — where relatively fewer working-age adults support more children and older people — are also the countries where access to handwashing is weakest. That makes poor hygiene not only a health problem, but also a pressure multiplier for families and public systems.

from plotnine import * scatter_df = merged_2022.to_pandas() corr_r = scatter_df["dep_ratio"].corr(scatter_df["handwashing_pct"]) # Annotate notable countries label_countries = ["Liberia","Qatar","Nigeria","India","Bangladesh","Ethiopia"] labels_df = scatter_df[scatter_df["country"].isin(label_countries)].copy() nudge = { "Liberia":(1.5,4),"Qatar":(-5,-5),"Nigeria":(1.5,3), "India":(1.5,-5),"Bangladesh":(1.5,3),"Ethiopia":(1.5,3), } labels_df["lx"] = labels_df.apply( lambda r: r["dep_ratio"] + nudge.get(r["country"],(1.5,0))[0], axis=1) labels_df["ly"] = labels_df.apply( lambda r: r["handwashing_pct"] + nudge.get(r["country"],(0,3))[1], axis=1) p3 = ( ggplot(scatter_df, aes(x="dep_ratio", y="handwashing_pct")) + geom_point(aes(color="region"), alpha=0.72, size=3) + geom_smooth(method="lm", color="#222", linetype="dashed", se=True, alpha=0.12) + geom_text(data=labels_df, mapping=aes(x="lx", y="ly", label="country"), size=8, fontstyle="italic") + scale_color_manual(values=REGION_COLOURS, name="Region") + theme_minimal() + labs(title=f"The Dependency-Hygiene Nexus (r = {corr_r:.2f})", x="Dependency Ratio", y="Handwashing Access (%)") ) p3.save("viz3_scatterplot.png", dpi=150)

The scatterplot quantifies the relationship: the correlation between total dependency ratio and handwashing access is about −0.77. As dependency burden rises, handwashing access tends to fall sharply. Figure 3 therefore links household hygiene to broader demographic stress, not just to household behaviour.

The country labels help anchor the pattern. Liberia sits close to the low-access, high-burden corner, while Qatar lies near the opposite extreme. Nigeria also stands out as a large-population case combining relatively weak access with a heavy dependency load. These are not isolated outliers; they sit inside a broader regional cluster.

Progress That Isn't Fast Enough

Figure 4 shows that progress has happened, but not at the speed required. Regions that started from stronger positions stayed high. The region facing the greatest need, Sub-Saharan Africa, improved only marginally across the period, leaving the global inequality gap largely intact.

from plotnine import * # Regional averages by year (2005-2022) ts_region = ( hw_all .join(region_lut, on="alpha_3_code", how="inner") .filter((pl.col("year") >= 2005) & (pl.col("year") <= 2022)) .group_by(["region","year"]) .agg([ pl.col("handwashing_pct").mean().alias("avg_hw"), pl.col("alpha_3_code").count().alias("n"), ]) .filter(pl.col("n") >= 3) .sort(["region","year"]) .to_pandas() ) # Manual label positions to avoid overlap endpoints = ts_region[ts_region["year"]==2022].copy() manual_y = { "North Africa & Middle East":93,"Europe & Central Asia":88, "East Asia & Pacific":82,"Latin America & Caribbean":76, "South Asia":69,"Sub-Saharan Africa":25, } endpoints["label_y"] = endpoints["region"].map(manual_y) endpoints["label"] = ( endpoints["region"] + "\n(" + endpoints["avg_hw"].round(1).astype(str) + "%)" ) p4 = ( ggplot(ts_region, aes(x="year", y="avg_hw", color="region", group="region")) + geom_line(size=1.15) + geom_point(size=2) + geom_text(data=endpoints, mapping=aes(x="year", y="label_y", label="label"), nudge_x=0.4, size=7.5, ha="left", show_legend=False) + scale_color_manual(values=REGION_COLOURS) + scale_x_continuous(breaks=list(range(2005,2023,2)), expand=(0.02,0,0.22,0)) + theme_minimal() + theme(legend_position="none", figure_size=(13,7)) + labs(title="Progress That Isn't Fast Enough", x="Year", y="Average Handwashing Access (%)") ) p4.save("viz4_time_series.png", dpi=150)

Between 2005 and 2022, every region improved to some extent. That matters. But the level and slope matter more than the mere direction of change. Figure 4 shows that regions already performing well stayed well ahead throughout the period.

Sub-Saharan Africa remains the most concerning case: it starts lowest, ends lowest, and moves only modestly across nearly two decades. In a region with rapid population growth, slow percentage gains can still mean very large numbers of people left without basic facilities.

South Asia presents the more hopeful counterexample. The gains associated with India and Bangladesh suggest that sustained investment in WASH infrastructure can shift the curve within one generation. That makes the stagnation elsewhere harder to explain away as inevitable.

What the Data Tells Us

The strongest version of this story is not that handwashing matters. That is obvious. It is that basic hygiene access travels with other life-shaping inequalities. The same countries that struggle to provide soap and water at home also tend to carry heavier demographic burdens and shorter life spans.

  • Figure 1 shows that low access is geographically concentrated rather than randomly distributed.
  • Figure 2 shows a large and persistent life expectancy gradient across handwashing-access groups.
  • Figure 3 shows that the relationship extends beyond health alone, linking weak hygiene access to higher dependency pressure.
  • Figure 4 shows that progress is possible, but too slow in the places where the need is greatest.

This is why the issue belongs in a public inequality report rather than a narrow WASH brief. Where basic infrastructure is missing, illness spreads more easily, care burdens rise, and life itself is shorter. The policy lesson is straightforward: handwashing access is not a side indicator. It is a visible marker of whether development is reaching households at all.

The world is not short of evidence, and it is not short of solutions. What 1.7 billion people are still waiting for is the political will to act on both.

Methodological Note

Data sources. Three files were used: unicef_indicator_1.csv (handwashing access, 120 countries, 2000–2024), unicef_indicator_2.csv (total dependency ratio, 228 countries, 1950–2023), and unicef_metadata.csv (country-level indicators, 211 countries, 1960–2024). All data originate from the UNICEF/WHO Joint Monitoring Programme and World Bank Development Indicators.

Joins. The 2022 cross-sectional dataset was constructed by inner-joining all three sources on alpha_3_code (ISO 3166-1 alpha-3 country codes) for year 2022, yielding 103 countries.

Quintile definition. Breaks at 15%, 40%, 65%, and 85% reflect the empirical distribution of 2022 handwashing values.

Limitations. The indicator 1 dataset covers only 120 countries. High-income OECD nations are largely absent. Correlations are Pearson coefficients and do not imply causation.

Limitations. The cross-sectional comparisons do not, by themselves, prove causation. Handwashing access is best interpreted here as a development marker that moves alongside water infrastructure, sanitation, health-system strength, and income. That limitation does not weaken the descriptive result; it clarifies what the charts are claiming.
